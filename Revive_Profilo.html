The Bridge of
Pixel and Light

Blending hand-drawn pixel art with real-time lighting technology in Unreal Engine 5 to redefine spatial cognition.

Researcher Shuhan Zhang
Prototype Revive
Programme BSc Interactive Media '25

Redefining
Perception

In contemporary video game design, 2.5D pixel art combines two-dimensional sprites with three-dimensional spatial environments. However, due to the inherent low resolution of pixel art, this style often lacks genuine volumetric cues.

Players depend heavily on lighting distribution and luminance contrast to judge depth and distinguish boundaries. This project explores lighting not merely as decoration, but as the primary structural language for how players read the scene.

Controlled
Environment

To isolate the impact of lighting, we conducted a structured analysis of player behavior across dynamic lighting states. Testing occurred under three distinct conditions: Day, Sunset, and Night.

Methodology

A 3x3 Latin Square counterbalanced nine participants across Day, Sunset and Night to eliminate order and learning effects.

Instruments

Post-session Likert questionnaires measured readability, difficulty and lantern dependency alongside recorded gameplay footage.

Telemetry

Automated UE5 Blueprint logging captured completion time, lantern utilisation, fragments found, and spatial backtracks.

Technical Execution

Engine & Rendering

Powered by Unreal Engine 5's Lumen global illumination. 2D pixel sprites were mapped precisely within 3D spatial geometry across background, midground, and foreground layers.

[ Visual 04: Blueprint logic ]

Lantern & Scanning

A handheld lantern utilizes a Blueprint-driven spherical collision volume to detect targets, providing critical local lighting and tracking player progression dynamically.

[ Visual 05: Fragment collision graph ]

Fragment Collection

Collision-triggered overlap events validate player interaction, increment collected fragments, trigger audio feedback, and hide objects to prevent redundant re-collection.

[ Visual 06: Lighting config array ]

Data & Environment

A custom S_LightingConfig structure drives Timeline-based Lerp transitions between Day, Sunset, and Night, while a live telemetry system records completion time and lantern usage for each trial.

The Paradox
of Darkness

Difficulty and immersion can be safely separated.

While the "Day" condition provided the best functional visibility, it paradoxically incurred the lowest rate of fragment collection. Conversely, the "Night" condition performed poorest on visibility, yet recorded full task accomplishment.

100%

of participants considered the "Night" condition the most challenging environment to navigate.

55.6%

simultaneously rated profound darkness as the most immersive experience.

Lower illumination forced players to transition from rapid visual sampling to systematic, tool-dependent exploration. Lighting in 2.5D pixel games actively shapes spatial cognition.

Design
Implications

Light is not simply atmosphere here — it is a core interactive bridge that shapes player focus, pacing and method of search, without a single change to level geometry.

Lighting as behavioural instrument

Darkness encourages measured, systematic search; abundant light invites faster, shallower traversal.

Balance efficiency against immersion

Dim lighting suits narrative high points; bright lighting suits tutorials and time-critical tasks.

Diegetic tools as guidance

The handheld lantern sustained near-perfect performance in the dark without breaking stylistic integrity.

Treat lighting as a dial, not a switch

Sunset's balanced profile — shortest completion time, strong atmosphere — shows the value of graded, adjustable illumination.

Reflection
& Next Steps

Choosing Unreal Engine 5 over Unity for its Lumen pipeline was the project's most consequential technical decision; treating the lantern's scan as an invisible collision volume, rather than a visible highlight, kept the recorded behaviour honest rather than prompted.

The study's main constraint is its sample size (N=9), which limits it to descriptive statistics. Future iterations plan a larger cohort (N≥30) for inferential testing, eye-tracking for objective attention data, and separating the lantern's lighting and scanning functions into independent conditions.

All testing followed University of York ethics approval, with anonymous, voluntary participation and informed consent throughout.
